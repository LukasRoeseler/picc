Concept Creep

In 2018, psychologists found something strange: when a category becomes rarer, people don't just notice less of it — their concept of it quietly expands. Show people fewer blue dots, and they start calling borderline purple dots "blue" too. Tell them the rules won't change, pay them to stay consistent, warn them in advance — the drift still creeps in.

This is a two-chapter replication of that effect (prevalence-induced concept change, Levari et al., 2018) — played on you, with your own responses as the data.

Your responses are sent anonymously for research purposes, as described in the consent you just gave.

One optional bonus round

Some people may resist this kind of drift more than others. One candidate: Need for Cognition — a trait describing how much someone enjoys effortful, deliberate thinking. The idea is that this bias comes from a low-effort, automatic recalibration of your judgment criterion; people who habitually think things through more might be more likely to notice the shifting rules and correct for it, showing less drift.

The 6 items below are an informal adaptation inspired by the Need for Cognition construct (Cacioppo & Petty, 1982) written for this game — not the validated instrument, and with one player this can't test any real correlation. It's just a personal data point to compare against your own drift scores above.
